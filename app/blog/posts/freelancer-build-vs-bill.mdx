Every hour I spend on a side project is an hour I'm not billing. At my rate, that's real money disappearing. And yet I keep building things that will never make a dollar.

This is the core tension of freelancing. You need to bill to survive, but you need to build to grow. Get the balance wrong and you either burn out or stagnate.

After three years of getting this wrong in various ways, here's how I think about it now.

## The Math Problem

Say you bill $75/hour. A side project that takes 40 hours costs you $3,000 in unbilled time. That's rent. That's not nothing.

So the side project needs to either:
- Make you more than $3,000 eventually
- Save you more than 40 hours in future work
- Open doors that wouldn't open otherwise

Most side projects do none of these things. They're just interesting. And "interesting" is expensive when you're freelancing.

## What I Actually Build Now

I've gotten ruthless about this. Here's what makes the cut:

**Tools that speed up client work.** I built a component library that matches how I structure projects. Took 20 hours upfront, saves me maybe 5 hours per project. After four projects, it's paid for itself. Everything after that is profit.

**Portfolio pieces that land clients.** Not generic "todo app" tutorials. Specific, polished things that demonstrate exactly the skills clients are paying for. One good portfolio piece has landed me more work than months of cold outreach.

**Learning that has immediate application.** I learned SwiftUI because a client might need iOS work. I didn't learn Rust because nobody's asking me to write Rust. The learning has to connect to revenue within a few months, or it's a hobby.

## What I Stopped Building

Side projects that are "good ideas" but have no path to my actual goals. The startup concepts. The SaaS dreams. The "wouldn't it be cool if" projects.

They're not bad ideas. They're just expensive distractions when you're trying to build a sustainable freelance practice.

I still have a list of things I want to build someday. But someday is when I have enough recurring revenue that I can afford to speculate. Right now, every hour needs to work.

## The Exception: When Billing Is Wrong

Sometimes the math flips. If you're burned out on client work, taking a week to build something fun might save your sanity. Sanity has value. Don't optimize yourself into misery.

And sometimes a "useless" project teaches you something you didn't know you needed. I built a dumb automation script last year that led to a whole new service offering. Couldn't have planned that.

The point isn't to never build anything speculative. It's to be honest about what you're doing. If it's exploration, call it exploration. Don't pretend it's strategic when it's really just procrastination in disguise.

## The Framework

When I'm considering a side project now, I ask:

1. **What's the real cost?** Hours times rate. Be honest.
2. **What's the realistic upside?** Not the dream scenario. The likely one.
3. **Is there a faster path to the same outcome?** Sometimes you can buy a tool instead of building it. Sometimes you can learn from a course instead of building from scratch.
4. **Am I avoiding something?** Side projects are great for dodging uncomfortable client conversations or difficult work. Catch yourself doing this.

If it still makes sense after those questions, build it. If not, get back to billing.

## The Real Answer

The freelancer's dilemma isn't actually about math. It's about identity.

Building things feels like being a real developer. Billing hours feels like being a contractor. Most of us got into this because we wanted to build, not invoice.

But here's what I've learned: you earn the right to build by being good at billing first. Get the business stable, then you have room to play. Try to skip that step and you end up broke, stressed, and building things nobody will ever see.

Bill first. Build with what's left. That's the order.
